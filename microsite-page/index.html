Apps compete for time the way casinos compete for pulls.

What this means (in plain language)

If something is “free,” the business still needs revenue. A common model is ads. Ads pay more when people spend more time inside the app.

So what’s the “attention economy”?

It’s the idea that human attention is limited — and companies compete to capture it. The more time you spend, the more opportunities they have to show ads or influence what you see.

Why slot machines are a good example

Slot machines don’t reward you every time. They reward you sometimes. That “maybe this time” feeling is powerful — and many apps copy it with refresh, scroll, and notifications.

What to notice

  • You don’t win most of the time.
  • But the chance of winning makes you want to try again.
  • Apps create the same feeling with “refresh,” “swipe,” and “new notifications.”
Key point: unpredictable rewards keep people coming back.

Slots are built to keep you pulling. Many feeds are built to keep you scrolling.

Casino design vs. app design

Different products, similar pattern.

Trigger

Casino: lights & sounds • Phone: pings, badges, boredom

Action

Casino: pull lever • Phone: scroll, refresh, swipe

Reward

Casino: small win sometimes • Phone: like, post, message sometimes

What you can do (simple, realistic)

You don’t need to delete everything. Add friction.

3 friction moves

  1. Turn off non-human notifications (likes, “suggested,” streaks).
  2. Set a timer before you open an app.
  3. Move apps off your home screen (make them harder to reach).

Quick reflection

Next time you catch yourself scrolling, ask:

  • What did I open this app for?
  • Did I get what I needed?
  • What do I actually want to do next?
